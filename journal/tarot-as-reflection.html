Tarot as Reflection, Not Fortune-Telling

June 10, 2026 · 5 min read

"So… will he come back?" The question arrives within the first five minutes of many first tarot sessions. I understand the longing behind it. We want certainty. We want to know the ending before we live the middle.

But in my practice, tarot is not a crystal ball. I do not predict fixed futures. I use the cards as a mirror — a structured way to explore what you already know beneath the surface, but haven't yet put into words.

How cards become conversation

Each card carries archetypal themes: change, loss, hope, conflict, renewal. When a card appears, we ask not "What will happen?" but "Where does this theme live in your life right now?"

The Star might invite a conversation about hope after exhaustion. The Tower might name a disruption you've been avoiding. The Empress might point toward self-care you've been denying yourself.

The card doesn't create the meaning — you do. The card simply opens a door.

For the curious skeptic

You are welcome to arrive skeptical. I expect it. Some of my most meaningful sessions have been with people who said, "I don't believe in this, but I'm willing to try." That willingness is enough.

We stay grounded. We stay practical. If a reading surfaces a career decision, we talk about your options. If it surfaces grief, we honour that grief. Tarot is the beginning of the conversation, not the end.

What tarot is not

It is not medical advice. It is not a substitute for therapy when deeper clinical support is needed. It is not a guarantee of outcomes. Anyone who tells you otherwise is not offering the kind of practice I believe in.
