When Your Child's Emotions Feel Bigger Than Yours

June 15, 2026 · 7 min read

Your child is crying — not a quiet tear, but the full-bodied wail that fills the room and, if you're honest, fills you with something too. Panic. Irritation. Shame. A memory of being told to stop.

Many parents tell me their child's emotions feel bigger than their own capacity to hold. This doesn't mean you're a bad parent. It often means your nervous system was never taught how to stay steady in the face of big feelings — yours or anyone else's.

What your child is really asking

Beneath the meltdown, the slammed door, the "I hate you" — there is usually a need. To be seen. To feel safe. To know that their feelings won't cost them your love. Children don't have the vocabulary adults do. They speak through behaviour.

When we react from our own triggered place — yelling back, shutting down, threatening — we accidentally confirm their fear: big feelings are dangerous.

What steadiness looks like

Steadiness doesn't mean perfect calm. It means returning. You might raise your voice — and then say, "I'm sorry I shouted. I'm here." You might need to step away for thirty seconds to breathe — and then come back.

Your repair is more powerful than your perfection.

When to seek support

If your child's distress is persistent, affecting school or sleep, or if you feel utterly depleted, professional support helps. Child healing sessions create a space for your child to express what they cannot yet say. Parenting guidance supports you — so you can show up without losing yourself.

You don't have to do this alone. Asking for help is not failure. It is one of the bravest things a parent can do.
