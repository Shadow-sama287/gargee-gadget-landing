Inner Child Work — What It Actually Means

June 1, 2026 · 6 min read

When I first mention "inner child work" to clients, I often see a flicker of uncertainty cross their face. Some imagine regression — sitting on a floor mat with crayons. Others worry it sounds too mystical, too Western, or simply too strange for someone like them.

So let me say plainly: inner child work is none of those things, necessarily. At its heart, it is the practice of meeting the younger parts of yourself that learned to survive — often by shrinking, pleasing, fighting, or disappearing — and offering them what they needed but did not receive.

Where the wounded child lives

Most of us carry patterns formed in childhood. Perhaps you learned that anger was dangerous, so you became agreeable. Perhaps you learned that your needs were a burden, so you stopped asking. Perhaps chaos at home taught your nervous system to stay alert, even decades later.

These are not character flaws. They are adaptations. Your inner child did what was necessary to get through. Inner child work honours that survival while gently asking: Is this still serving me?

What it looks like in session

In my practice, inner child work is grounded and conversational. We might explore a recurring emotional reaction — the sudden sting of rejection, the inability to rest without guilt — and trace it back to its roots. We might use visualisation, journaling, or simply compassionate dialogue.

The goal is not to "fix" your childhood. That cannot be undone. The goal is to reparent yourself now — with the wisdom, boundaries, and tenderness you may not have received then.

A note for the skeptical

You do not need to believe in anything esoteric. You only need to recognise that the adult you are today was shaped by the child you once were. That is not poetry. It is psychology.

If something in this article resonates, you are welcome to explore it further — in your own journal, in conversation with someone you trust, or in a session. There is no rush. Healing, as I often say, is not always a straight line.
